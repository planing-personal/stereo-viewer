What Is Stereo Photography?

Stereo photography (stereoscopy) creates the illusion of three-dimensional depth by presenting two slightly offset images — one for each eye — mimicking the way human binocular vision naturally perceives the world.

📷 Twin-lens cameras 👁️ Binocular vision 🖼️ Side-by-side pairs

How It Works

Your eyes are roughly 2.5 inches (6.5 cm) apart, so each eye sees the world from a slightly different angle. The brain combines these two perspectives into a single image with depth perception.

Stereo photographs capture this by taking two photos simultaneously from two lenses spaced the same distance apart. When each eye views its corresponding image, the brain reconstructs the depth illusion.

How to View Stereo Photos

Two common techniques let you see the 3D effect without any special equipment.

∥

Parallel (Free-View)

Also called "wall-eyed" viewing

Relax your eyes and look through the image as if focusing on a distant object. The two images will drift apart, then a third image will appear in the center in 3D.

Cross-Eyed

Images are swapped left↔right

Slightly cross your eyes so that your left eye looks at the right image and your right eye looks at the left image. The images will converge into a 3D view in the center.

Pro tip

Start with the parallel method — it's more natural for most people. If you see a reversed-depth effect (things that should pop out go inward), try the cross-eyed method instead, or swap the left and right images. Our Stereo Viewer has a handy Swap L/R button.
